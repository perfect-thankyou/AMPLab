These 4 habits do more than keep your body healthy. They build skills that help you in every part of your life.

Here's what researchers found...

⚡

Energy

The fuel to keep going toward what matters to you
In one experiment, young adults were given just 2 extra servings of fruits and vegetables a day. Within 2 weeks, they reported feeling more alive, more motivated, and more curious about life — from food alone.
🥦 What researchers found about food:

Many people believe sugar gives you energy. But a large review of studies found the opposite — sugar actually increases tiredness and lowers alertness within 30-60 minutes. Whole foods, protein, and water give your body steady fuel instead of a spike and crash. Even mild dehydration is linked to lower energy and harder thinking.

🏃🏽 What researchers found about exercise:

Teens who are more physically active report more energy, better overall health, and more enjoyment of life — and the effect gets stronger the more active you are.

😴 What researchers found about sleep:

In an experiment, healthy teens (ages 14-17) were limited to 6.5 hours of sleep for just 5 nights. After only a few days, they reported more fatigue, more confusion, and more anger. That amount of sleep is what millions of teens get on school nights.

These aren't small differences. Your body is designed to feel alive and energized. When you fuel it, move it, and rest it — that energy shows up.

🧠

Focus

A clear mind to make good choices and stay on track
Your brain uses more energy during your teen years than at any other time in your life. That means how you take care of it right now matters more than ever.
🏃🏽 What researchers found about exercise:

In a study of 400,000 teens, the fittest ones had sharper attention, faster thinking, and better memory. They performed better — not because they tried harder, but because their brains worked better.

🥦 What researchers found about food:

Teens who eat more fruits, vegetables, and whole foods have better attention and memory. In a study of 46,000 teens across 42 countries, healthier eating was linked to better performance in school. Ultra-processed food was linked to the opposite.

😴 What researchers found about sleep:

Good sleep habits in 5th grade predicted better thinking in 9th grade — 4 years later. And getting enough sleep helps reduce the harm that too much sitting and screen time can do to your brain.

📵 What researchers found about screen time:

Researchers found that a lot of mindless scrolling trains your brain to chase quick rewards, which makes it harder to stay focused on things that take time and effort.

💪

Follow-Through

Trusting yourself to do what you say you'll do
Researchers studying teens found that physical fitness predicted something surprising: not just health — but whether a teen believed they could follow through on their goals in general. Being active builds confidence that carries into everything you do.
🥦 What researchers found about food:

In a 2-week experiment, the group that ate more fruits and vegetables didn't just feel more energized — they also felt more motivated to go after what mattered to them. What you eat affects how driven you feel.

😴 What researchers found about sleep:

A review of 61 studies found that better sleep is directly linked to better self-control — the ability to choose what you really want over what feels easy right now.

✅ What it all adds up to:

Every time you make a health choice that matches what you want for your future — choosing real food over junk, getting to bed on time, getting your body moving — you're training yourself to trust your own word. Each choice builds on the last.

🏔️

Perseverance

Keeping going when things get hard
When teens learn to see difficulty as a sign that something MATTERS to them (instead of a sign to give up), they stick with their goals longer and do better. In one study, teens who learned this got higher grades, better test scores, missed fewer days of school, and felt less depressed — and the effects were still seen 2 years later.

Health is one of the best places to practice this — because you face these choices every single day: choosing water over soda, eating real food when junk food is easier, getting to bed on time, pushing through a workout, putting your phone down.

None of these are easy in the moment. That's the point.

And perseverance doesn't mean being perfect. It means that when you have a rough week, or you fall off track, or you're not seeing results yet — you come back to it. You keep choosing. The fact that it's hard doesn't mean you're failing. It means it matters to you.

Every day you keep showing up for your health is practice for showing up in every other part of your life.
